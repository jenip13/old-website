Data Science Internship at the Canadian Space Agency

During the summer of 2019 (late April to late August 2019), I was an intern at the Data Expertise Center of the Canadian Space Agency (CSA).

My main project was to implement a pipeline to process and analyze scanned images acquired from the Alouette-I satellite, the first Canadian Satellite launched into space. The processing library was written in Python3, particularly using the open-cv, numpy, matplotlib and pandas libraries. Documentation was generated using Sphinx. Some analysis and visualization modules were written in R. The code will soon be available to the public. Challenges with the dataset includes its noisyness, its nonuniformity and its complexity.

A side project was demonstrating the feasibiliy of using CKAN, an open-source data portal platform, to implement CSA's future open data portal. I implemented a CKAN data portal web instance on a Linux virtual machine. To implement features requested by CSA's Open Data team, I customized CKAN's HTML and CSS files as well as wrote Python2 modules.

I also was involved in CSA's participation for the Space Apps Challenge (NASA’s global hackathon involving challenges on space datasets). I assisted in many meetings and suggested ways to improve challenges to render them more accessible and attractive to students. I wrote Python3 and R scripts to clean certain datasets.

Milo: the brain-controlled wheelchair

During Winter 2019 (January to May), as part of the Neurotech McGill design team, I lead the signal processing subteam to build a brain-controlled wheelchair.

The wheelchair is controlled by imagined movements (i.e. to turn right, a user imagines their right hand) detected via electroencephalography (EEG) processed on a computer. It was built by 35 students via 6 subteams including research, data collection, signal processing, machine learning, software and robotics. The mandate of the signal processing subteam was to implement a protocol to clean the raw EEG signal and extract useful features. Thus, using the raw EEG data acquired by the data collection subteam, I lead a team of six students to generate useful features for the machine learning subteam. Most of the signal processing was done in Python3, using the numpy and scipy libraries. The source code for the entire project is available to the public.

Our project won first place at the International NeurotechX Student Competition's Open Challenge. A paper on the project that I co-wrote with the other leads will be presented at the 2019 IEEE International Conference on Systems, Man, and Cybernetics (SMC).

Our project has garnered attention from both English and French provincial media outlets. We have conducted radio interviews with CBC and CJAD Montreal . We have conducted a televised interview with CTV Montreal. We have also conducted interviews with newspapers under the Quebecor umbrella, including 24H, Journal de Montreal and TVA Nouvelles .

MR-Ai: Swipe left on your bad MRI scans

During June 2018, I co-developed a desktop application with an integrated convolutional neural network to determine whether a patient had moved during an MRI scan.

The project was done through the Ai for Social Good Summer Lab, a 6-week long program to train female-identifying University students in applied Machine Learning, design and prototype development. While the first few weeks were dedicated to learning, the last few weeks were dedicated to building a project that uses Machine Learning to solve a problem.

We artificially created noise on a public MRI datasets through rigid transformations and blurring. We then trained our machine learning model (a convolutional neural network with five convolutional layers and three fully connected layers) on the resulting data to detect noise in MRI scans. The project was implemented using Python3, particularly the numpy, scipy and PyTorch libraries. We also implemented a simple user interface using Tkinter to separate MRI scans into separate folders based on whether noise was detected. The source code for the project is publicly available.

At the end of the project, we presented our project at a demo night, where we were awarded first place by Google DeepMind. Our project was also selected to be presented at the 2018 ACM Canadian Celebration of Women in Computing Conference as well as the 2018 Women in Machine Learning Workshop.

2016 Conference for Diversity in Engineering

I was the programs coordinator for the 2016 Conference for Diversity in Engineering (CDE2016), a student-lead conference bringing together Engineering student leaders from across Canada to discuss equity issues such as accessibility/ universal design and lack of representation from certain groups.

As the programs coordinator, I lead a team of three volunteers to reach out to potential speakers and panelists and follow-up with them mostly through emails and phonecalls. Our tasks included scheduling, arranging transportation, obtaining biographies and booking accommodation. We also had to develop questions for five of the six the panel discussions. Because we had no budget for speakers fees, I also negotiated with some speakers to exchange their fees for other perks such as advertising.

For the 3-day conference, we had to organize the logistics around hosting 60 keynotes, speakers, panelists and workshop faciliators. Our four keynotes include Julie Payette, a former Canadian Space Agency astronaut. We had nine workshops, which ranged from Creative Thinking Tools and Techniques to Unconscious Bias. We had five panels, which ranged from Indigenous perspectives in STEM to Transitioning from University to Industry. Our ten talks ranged from public speaking to being queer in the workplace.

McGill Women in Computer Science

I am currently a co-president of McGill Women in Computer Science (McWiCS) and I was previously its events coordinator

The mandate of McWiCS is to cultivate a warm, open-minded, diverse, and inclusive community of techies dedicated to supporting and encouraging gender minorities in the field of computer science. While our biggest event of the year is our annual hackathon, we also have smaller events such as talks, workshops and industry tours. As the events coordinator, I have organized those smaller events. I established sponsorship relationships with new companies. In addition, I helped extensively with food logistics for the 2019 McWiCS Hackathon.

As the current McWiCS co-president, my goal is to increase our outreach as well as to increase our budget. I am seeking to make the organization more welcoming to queer women and non-binary people as well as to organize events with clubs that McWiCS has not collaborated with before. In addition, I hope to increase our hackathon turnout, from 150 to 250 students. I have currently personally raised 2,600 $. The Hackathon subteam and I have raised 15,000$.

Research in computational Bioengineering

During 2017 and 2018, I completed summer projects as an undergraduate research assistant at computational bioengineering laboratories.

From January to August 2017, I worked at Professor Dan Nicolau’s Biological Microfluidics Laboratory, where I investigated the use of rectifying structures in a parallel computing method involving E. coli.

From January to August 2018, I worked at Professor Brandon Xia's Computational and Systems Biology Lab I extracted, processed, and combined several gigabyte-sized datasets via Bash shell scripting, Python scripting and SQLite. By selectively merging the datasets, I uncovered new insights regarding protein-protein interactions.

Both projects were funded through Undergraduate Student Research Awards from the Natural Sciences and Engineering Research Council of Canada (NSERC). The projects were done in the context of McGill University's Summer Undergraduate Research in Engineering (SURE) and were presented at SURE poster sessions.
